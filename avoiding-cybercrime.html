Avoiding Cybercrime

Listed below are various types of cybercrime, what they are, how they affect you, and how you can stop them.

We take many precautions to avoid Cybercrime. You should as well.

Cybercrime is serious, and of course illegal. It is harmful (usually not in a physical way but some types can be), can damage your reputation, data, and much more. Cybercrime is no fun, which is why we're going to show you the most common types of Cybercrime, how they work, what they do to affect you, and how to avoid them.

1. Malware/Virus Attacks

A malware attack is a common cyberattack where malware (malicious software) executes unauthorized actions on the victim's system. The malware (also known as a virus) encompasses many specific types of attacks such as ransomware, spyware, key-loggers, and more.
To avoid Malware, we suggest using MalwareBytes (for windows) or ClamAV (for Linux).

2. DDoS Attacks

Distributed Network Attacks are often referred to as Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks. The DDoS attack will send multiple requests to the attacked web resource – with the goal of exceeding the website's (or network's) network capacity, forcing the website to stop functioning properly.
To mitigate DDoS attacks (on your website or server), head over to this page.

3. Computer Hacking

Hacking is when a person intentionally gains unauthorized access to a computer or server. Usually, hackers install malware, ransomware, spyware, or access and steal sensitive information such as banking details, credit card info, documents, and much more. Hacking is also when a hacker gains unauthorized access to an online account of yours (ex: YouTube Account, Social Media Account, Email Account, etc)
To avoid getting hacked, simply don't visit shady websites, never reveal personal information to random online people, and always use an anti-malware software. Click here to learn how to further secure your online accounts.

4. Cryptojacking

Crypto Mining aka Cryptojacking is when a user intentionally gains unauthorized access to a server or computer in order to install special software that uses up the computer's/server's resources (usually CPU or GPU) to mine cryptocurrencies such as Bitcoin, Monero, etc. If your computer has been very slow lately, this may be why.
To avoid cryptojacking, simply don't download software from a shady website, and use an anti-virus.

5. Online Scams

Internet fraud is a type of cybercrime fraud or deception which makes use of the Internet and could involve hiding of information or providing incorrect information for the purpose of tricking victims out of money, property, and inheritance.
To avoid online website scams, ensure the website you are visiting has a TLS (more often referred to as SSL) Certificate, use a secure DNS resolver service, and use an anti-virus.

6. Phishing

Phishing is a type of social engineering where an attacker sends a fraudulent message designed to trick a human victim into revealing sensitive information to the attacker or to deploy malicious software on the victim's infrastructure like ransomware. To avoid email phishing, make sure you choose the right email provider, and have anti-spam software installed like SpamAssassin.

7. Cyberstalking

Cyberstalking is the use of the Internet or other electronic means to stalk or harass an individual, group, or organization. It may include false accusations, defamation, slander and libel. It may also include monitoring, identity theft, threats, vandalism, solicitation for sex, doxing, or blackmail. To avoid Cyberstalking, don't talk to random people who seem shady, use an anti-virus, use a VPN, and never give away your identity. If it's gotten insanely serious, contact the local authorities.

8. Identity Theft

Identity theft occurs when someone uses another person's personal identifying information, like their name, identifying number, or credit card number, without their permission, to commit fraud or other crimes. To recover from Identity theft, visit https://www.identitytheft.gov/ and call the IRS Customer Service number immediately.

9. Social Engineering

In the context of information security, social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. This differs from social engineering within the social sciences, which does not concern the divulging of confidential information.
