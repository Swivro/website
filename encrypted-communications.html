Encrypted Messengers

Most instant messenger apps, like WhatsApp, violate your privacy. We'll recommend encrypted privacy-respecting messengers.

Need to communicate on the internet securely without prying eyes?

Many of the most popular instant messengers on the internet simply violate your privacy, have poor security, or lack important features. That includes WhatsApp, Viber, and Telegram. Below, we'll recommend some extremely secure & private ways to communicate with other people on the internet without prying eyes.

1. Session App

Session is a private messaging app that fully encrypts your communications, and ensures your messaging activities leave no digital trail behind. Conversations in Session are automatically end-to-end encrypted, something not seen with Telegram, Discord, Slack, & more. When you use Session, the identities of the people communicating are also protected. Session keeps your communication private, secure, and anonymous. When using Session, your messages are sent to their destinations through a decentralized "onion routing" network similar to Tor, using a system called onion requests. Onion requests protect user privacy by ensuring that no single server ever knows a message’s origin or destination.

2. Element

Like Session, Element is a very popular instant messenger. Unlike Session, Element is powered by Matrix, which is an open standard and communication protocol for real-time communication. Today, Element is a modern privacy-focused alternative to Discord, IRC, and Mfst. Teams. Like Session, Matrix utilizes end-to-end encryption by default, however, we would like to note that Element has not received an independent code audit and security analysis for some time now. Element also does not provide a transparency report, and does not support a second factor of authentication. Though Element has been around longer than Session and has some more features, Session is recommended over Element for enhanced privacy, boosted anonymity, and potentially stronger security.

3. Threema

Threema is an open-source end-to-end encrypted instant messaging app for iOS and Android. The software claims to be privacy-respecting, as it does not require a phone number. Though Threema is considered privacy-respecting and secure (it has been independently audited in October, 2020), it's not the best option if you're looking to communicate anonymously on the internet, with the main reason being, to use Threema, you must make a one-time payment to them via MasterCard, Visa Card, PayPal, and Bitcoin. None of these payment methods are truly anonymous (even Bitcoin), meaning Threema may be able to easily trace back the identity of the user. If they accepted Monero, perhaps it would be a different story.
