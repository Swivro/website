The Tor Network

We'll discuss how the Tor Browser & Tor Network functions, how it makes you anonymous, the security risks, and how to stay the most secure.

Tor, short for The Onion Router, is an anonymity network powered by the Tor Project and thousands of volunteers across the universe. The Tor Network does not make you completely anonymous, but definitely boosts your anonymity. You can never be 100% anonymous on the internet. The easiest and most safe way to access websites via the Tor Network is to use the official Tor Browser from torproject.org. Do not download "Tor Browser" from any other sources. The way Tor works is, once you go to a normal website via the Tor Browser, your connection is passed to a Tor Guard Node, which is the first server your traffic goes to. It is then passed to one or more relay servers, where your traffic is relayed from one server to another, and then is finally passed to the Exit Node, which is the node that reaches out to the internet. The exit node fetches your requested content, then passes it back through the relay of servers to your machine. Tor Nodes (exit/relay/guard nodes) are mostly ran by volunteers and sadly the government. 

Simplified Explanation:

Guard Node: The first server/node that your Tor Browser connects to. This is the only Tor Node that can see what your IP address is.

Relay Node: These type of Tor Nodes are nodes that receive and pass traffic to other relay nodes. They relay your web traffic from server to server. Relay Nodes cannot see your IP address, and just see the IP address of other relay nodes (or exit/guard nodes) that they are passing traffic to or are receiving traffic from.

Exit Node: The final Tor Node that your web traffic/request reaches. The exit node is the node that actually fetches your requested content from the clearnet or dark web, then passes it back through all of the relay nodes and the guard node, to your machine.

Even when you are using Tor, if one of the relay nodes you are using is ran by the government, that malicious (government-ran) relay node should not be able to see what’s going on, as all traffic passed to it is encrypted, then passed to another relay node (or exit node). The Tor Browser also sets a certain screen size (which is why you may see a border around website content), and useragent, so that all people using the Tor Browser have a common fingerprint, and no Tor users can be uniquely identified. When you visit a .onion site through Tor, your traffic is passed through more relay nodes, for increased anonymity. Tor is a very good thing, and you can even route all of your internet traffic through Tor by using Torifier, Tallow, or some other programs, however we suggest sticking with the Tor Browser, as routing all of your traffic through Tor can de-anonymize you since you would be able to be uniquely identified, despite your IP address being hidden. The dangerous part of Tor is when you are using an exit or guard node controlled by the government. This is because, if you are connected to a Guard node that is controlled by the government, then the government will be able to see your real IP address. Most people do not recommend using Tor in conjunction with a VPN, but we do, because if the government contacts your ISP and tells them to prevent you from accessing any Tor Guard Nodes besides ones owned by the government, then the government will be able to see your IP address and the fact that you’re using Tor. With the right VPN provider (we recommend ProtonVPN or Mullvad), they will not allow the government to do such a thing, and if you happen to connect to a government-operated guard node, the government will see the VPN IP address and not your actual home IP address. People say "well by using a VPN you're just changing who can see your IP address, now your VPN provider can see it". Would you really trust your ISP over a privacy-focused VPN company like ProtonVPN or Mullvad? You could even set up your own VPN using WireGuard or OpenVPN. Use a VPN.

Great, so now you have a VPN on, and you’re more safe from any malicious government-operated guard nodes, but now exit nodes are the dangerous part, because if the government is operating an exit node and your traffic goes through that exit node, then the government will be able to see what sites you are visiting, as the exit node is the only node to reach out to the internet to fetch your requested web content. There's not much you can do about this if the sites you are visiting are clearnet sites, but as long as you don't do anything illegal, stupid, or visit any shady websites, you should be fine. Try to always visit onionsites rather than clearnet sites, because when you visit an onionsite, you don't need to use an exit node, and since you have a VPN on, this means the government will not be able to see what you're doing and your home IP address is hidden even from the guard node. Essentially; only use the Tor Browser (do not route your internet traffic through Tor then use Chrome), make sure you only download Tor Browser from torproject.org, use a VPN (we either suggest ProtonVPN, Mullvad, or your own self-hosted WireGuard Tunnel), and only access onionsites. This gives you a great amount of anonymity and ensures the government or any other malicious Tor Nodes cannot see or who you are and what you're doing. To answer the question, as long as you take the necessary precautions which we just talked about above, you will be nearly fully anonymous while using Tor Browser!
